The four accounts your money can live in

If you have a 401(k) but have never really understood it, start here. Plain English, one idea at a time, nothing to memorize. Tap anything to open it.

Read this part if nothing else

An account is a container, not an investment.

A 401(k) or an IRA isn't a thing you buy — it's a box you put investments inside. The box decides the tax rules; the stuff inside the box (funds, stocks) decides how it grows. Two people can own the exact same fund — one in a Roth, one in a brokerage — and keep wildly different amounts after taxes. So the question isn't only what to buy. It's which box to put it in first.

*This is the classic "traditional" 401(k). Many plans also offer a Roth 401(k), where it flips: taxed now, tax-free later.

What it actually is

A retirement account your employer sets up. You pick a percentage of your paycheck, and it's pulled out automatically before you ever see it — that "set it and forget it" auto-pilot is why most people end up invested without trying.

The best part

Many employers match part of what you put in — say, 50¢ for every $1 up to a limit. That's a 50% return before the market does anything. Always grab the full match; skipping it is leaving pay on the table.

The catches

  • You can only invest in the menu of funds your plan offers — not anything you want.
  • Touch it before age 59½ and you usually owe taxes + a 10% penalty.
  • Traditional 401(k)s force you to start withdrawing (and paying tax) at age 73 — called RMDs.

What it actually is

A retirement account you open at a brokerage (Fidelity, Vanguard, Schwab). You fund it with money you've already paid tax on — so the deal is: pay the IRS now, and every dollar of growth comes out completely tax-free in retirement.

The best part

Decades of gains, never taxed. It's the best deal in the tax code if you expect to be in a similar-or-higher tax bracket later — and it has no RMDs, so it can grow untouched your whole life. You can also pull out what you contributed (not the growth) anytime, penalty-free — a quiet emergency backstop.

The catches

  • Earn too much and you can't contribute directly (see limits below).
  • Pulling out the earnings early can trigger taxes + a 10% penalty.
  • You're using after-tax dollars, so it "costs" more up front than a traditional account.

2026 limits

Add $7,500/yr Age 50+: +$1,100

Income to contribute fully phases out: single $153k–$168k, married-jointly $242k–$252k (2026). Above that, contributions shrink to zero. This $7,500 is shared with a Traditional IRA — it's one combined limit.

What it actually is

The mirror image of a Roth, opened the same way (by you, at a brokerage). You may get a tax deduction this year for what you put in, the money grows untaxed, and you pay ordinary tax later when you pull it out. Think: tax break now, bill later.

The best part

Lowers your taxable income today. Most useful if you think you'll be in a lower tax bracket in retirement than you are now, or you want the deduction this year. It's also the landing spot when people roll over an old 401(k) after leaving a job.

The catches

  • Withdrawals are fully taxed as income — the tax bill is just deferred, not erased.
  • RMDs at 73: the IRS makes you start withdrawing (and paying).
  • Early withdrawals before 59½ generally cost taxes + a 10% penalty.

Anyone with earned income can contribute — but the deduction is what phases out at higher incomes if you're covered by a work plan. Shares the $7,500 limit with a Roth IRA.

What it actually is

A plain taxable investment account you open yourself. No retirement strings attached — you invest after-tax money and the government taxes your dividends and gains along the way, but in exchange you get total freedom.

The best part

No contribution limits, no income limits, and no age rules — take money out whenever you want, for anything. It's the right box for goals before retirement (a house, a few years out) and for investing more once your tax-advantaged accounts are full.

The catches

  • No tax break — you owe tax on gains and dividends, even some years you don't sell.
  • Holding over a year gets a lower long-term capital-gains rate; selling quickly is taxed higher.
  • Easy access cuts both ways — nothing stops you from cashing out at the worst time.

Limits

Step 4

Check yourself

Three quick questions. Tap an answer — it tells you why right away.

1. Your friend says "I bought a 401(k)." What's off about that?
An account is a container, not an investment. The 401(k) is the box; funds go inside. That one idea unlocks the rest.
2. Your employer matches 401(k) contributions. What should you usually do first?
The match is free money — often an instant 50–100% return. Grabbing the full match almost always comes before anything else.
3. Which box lets your growth come out completely tax-free in retirement?
A Roth is taxed going in, then growth and withdrawals come out tax-free. Traditional is the reverse; a brokerage is taxed along the way.
