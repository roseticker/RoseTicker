What the company actually does

Think of Nebius as a landlord for AI computing power. Training modern AI needs thousands of expensive Nvidia GPUs; Nebius owns the data centers and rents that capacity out, so customers don't have to build it themselves.

The core business

Its AI cloud is ~98% of revenue. It's racing to build capacity — data centers in New Jersey, the UK, Israel and Finland — targeting roughly 1 gigawatt of total capacity (a huge amount of power). It has signed $20B+ in customer contracts, headlined by a $17.4B deal with Microsoft plus work with Meta.

Why people are excited: demand for AI computing is booming and supply is scarce. Nebius is one of the few independent providers scaling fast, with Nvidia as a backer and shareholder.

The "hidden" extra assets

Beyond the cloud, Nebius owns stakes in several businesses it could sell to help fund growth:

Avride · self-driving / delivery robots · 83% · ~$3.4B ClickHouse · database company · 28% · ~$6B Toloka · AI data labeling · majority · Bezos-backed TripleTen · tech-reskilling edtech
In plain terms: these stakes are a potential piggy bank. Selling pieces could raise cash without borrowing — but their values aren't guaranteed.

The financial picture (Q1 2026)

Growth is explosive and, unlike many AI startups, the core business is becoming profitable on an operating basis — but it's spending enormous sums to build data centers.

The key tension: Nebius plans to spend $20–25B this year while bringing in ~$3B of revenue. That gap must be funded by customer prepayments, selling assets, borrowing, or issuing new shares. Whether it can keep funding this without hurting shareholders is the whole debate.

Bull case vs. bear case

Both sides agree growth is real. They disagree on whether today's price already assumes near-perfect execution.

👍 Bull case

  • Explosive, real demandRevenue up ~684% YoY with $20B+ in signed contracts, including Microsoft.
  • Nvidia-backed & well-positionedOne of few independent AI-cloud players scaling fast, with Nvidia as an investor.
  • Core unit is profitable-ish~40% adjusted-EBITDA margin in the cloud business — rare for a hyper-growth name.
  • Hidden asset valueStakes in ClickHouse (~$6B), Avride (~$3.4B) and Toloka could fund growth.
  • Index inclusionJoining the Nasdaq-100 brings forced buying from index funds.

👎 Bear case

  • Very expensive stockTrades around 60x+ trailing sales — priced for years of flawless growth.
  • Enormous spending$20–25B capex vs ~$3B revenue; needs constant new funding.
  • Dilution / debt riskFunding that gap may mean issuing shares or taking on debt.
  • Customer concentrationA few giant deals (Microsoft, Meta) drive much of the backlog.
  • Whipsaw volatilityCan swing ~$20+ a day and falls hard in AI-sector selloffs.
  • Overcapacity riskIf AI compute demand cools, all that capacity could sit underused.

Risks, ranked

This is a high-volatility, richly-valued stock. The biggest risks aren't about whether the business is growing — it is — but about price and funding.

HIGH
Valuation leaves little margin for error. At ~60x+ sales, even strong results can disappoint if they're not spectacular. A growth wobble can mean a big price drop.
HIGH
Funding the build-out. Spending $20–25B on roughly $3B of revenue depends on prepayments, asset sales, debt, or new shares — any of which can pressure the stock or dilute owners.
HIGH
AI-sector sentiment. NBIS trades with the whole AI/chip complex. The June 5, 2026 semiconductor selloff dragged it down sharply regardless of its own news.
MEDIUM
Customer concentration. A handful of mega-deals drive the backlog; losing or delaying one would hurt disproportionately.
MEDIUM
Execution & overcapacity. Building gigawatts of data centers on schedule is hard; if demand softens, expensive capacity could sit idle.
MEDIUM
Daily volatility. Moves of ~$20 a day are normal — uncomfortable if you need stable value or might sell in a panic.
