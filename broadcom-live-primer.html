What Broadcom is

Broadcom runs two businesses under one roof — and the combination is the strategy. One captures the explosive growth of AI; the other delivers steady, predictable cash.

~58%

Semiconductor Solutions

Custom AI chips, networking, broadband, wireless and storage silicon. This is the growth engine, led by AI.

~42%

Infrastructure Software

VMware, plus security (Symantec) and mainframe (CA) software. Mostly recurring subscriptions — the stabilizer.

Approximate share of total revenue. The software share rose sharply after the 2023 VMware acquisition.

Why the structure matters: semiconductors are cyclical (boom-and-bust), while software subscriptions arrive whether or not the chip cycle is hot. Holding both smooths earnings and funds the heavy R&D needed to stay ahead in chips — a balance few rivals have.

How it got here

Today's Broadcom was assembled by CEO Hock Tan through a decade of disciplined acquisitions — Avago bought the original Broadcom in 2016 (and took its name), then added Brocade, CA Technologies, Symantec's enterprise unit, and finally VMware for $69B. The result is a portfolio of category leaders rather than a single product line. It is fabless: it designs the chips and TSMC manufactures them.

Where you already encounter it

Broadcom components sit in Wi-Fi routers, set-top boxes, and smartphones — Apple is among its largest customers. But the investment story today is concentrated in the data center: the custom AI chips and networking that hyperscalers (the largest cloud operators — Google, Meta, Microsoft, Amazon) depend on.

HQ: Palo Alto, CA CEO: Hock Tan (since 2006) ~37,000 employees Did a 10-for-1 stock split in 2024

The AI engine: custom silicon + networking

Broadcom's role in AI is distinct from Nvidia's. Rather than selling one powerful chip to everyone, it co-designs bespoke chips for individual customers, and supplies the networking that ties thousands of chips together.

Custom accelerators ("XPUs")

The biggest tech firms increasingly design their own AI chips to control performance, power and cost — but they rely on Broadcom for the hard engineering (high-speed interconnects, memory, advanced packaging). Broadcom holds an estimated ~70% share of this custom-silicon design market.

Networking — the other half of AI

As AI clusters scale to hundreds of thousands of chips, moving data between them becomes the bottleneck. Broadcom dominates high-end Ethernet switching (its Tomahawk and Jericho families), which the industry is adopting over Nvidia's proprietary InfiniBand to avoid vendor lock-in. Networking is now roughly 40% of AI revenue, up from ~33% a year earlier.

The number that drives the stock: management guides AI semiconductor revenue to ~$56B in FY2026 (~180% growth) and over $100B in FY2027, supported by a $73B AI order backlog (part of a $162B total backlog). Hitting those targets is the core of the bull case; missing them is the core of the bear case.

The software base: VMware

The $69B VMware deal transformed Broadcom into roughly a 40%-software company. It's less glamorous than AI chips, but it's the financial bedrock.

VMware's core product, Cloud Foundation (VCF), lets large enterprises run a private cloud — their own data centers operated with cloud-like flexibility — as an alternative to renting from Amazon or Microsoft. Broadcom scrapped one-time licenses in favor of annual subscriptions and pushed its biggest customers onto full-stack bundles, lifting revenue per customer.

Higher prices, real friction

The aggressive repricing sparked a backlash. Major customers — including AT&T, Fidelity and Tesco — have pursued lawsuits, and some smaller clients are migrating to rivals like Nutanix. Exiting VMware is slow (often 18–36 months), which has helped Broadcom hold pricing power for now. Software revenue still grew ~9–13% year-over-year, suggesting higher spend per remaining customer is outweighing churn.

How to read it: this segment is a "monetize the installed base" play — squeeze more recurring revenue from customers who find it hard to leave. It generates the dependable cash that funds the semiconductor R&D race. The risk is that, over years, the price hikes accelerate defections.

A payout ratio near 40% means the dividend is well-covered and has room to keep rising. The trade-off: a chunk of earnings still leans on the AI cycle and can include non-cash accounting swings, so quarter-to-quarter profit can be lumpy.

On valuation

At roughly $2 trillion, Broadcom is priced for years of strong AI growth — a premium valuation. That's not automatically "too expensive," but it means execution matters: if growth merely meets (rather than beats) expectations, the shares can still fall. The stock is also more volatile than the broad market (beta ~1.4).

Competitive position & risks

Broadcom sits alongside Nvidia, AMD and Marvell as a core "AI infrastructure" stock, but its profile differs: more diversified, software-backed, and focused on custom silicon plus networking.

Strengths

  • Picks-and-shovels exposureSupplies chips and networking to nearly every AI builder.
  • Revenue visibilityMulti-year customer programs and a $73B AI backlog.
  • Dual engineSoftware subscriptions cushion semiconductor cyclicality.
  • Cash generation$10B+ quarterly free cash flow; growing dividend; buybacks.

What could go wrong

  • Premium valuationA lot of future growth is already in the price.
  • Customer concentrationA few hyperscalers drive most AI revenue.
  • Supply chainReliant on TSMC and scarce advanced packaging (CoWoS).
  • Software defectionsVMware repricing could accelerate churn over time.

Risks, ranked

HIGH
Valuation depends on the AI build-out continuing. If hyperscaler spending slows or results only meet expectations, the premium can compress quickly.
MEDIUM
Concentration. A handful of customers (and one foundry, TSMC) underpin the AI business; any one stumbling has outsized impact.
MEDIUM
Geopolitics. Roughly 17–20% of sales touch China; tighter export controls could bite.
MEDIUM
Software backlash. Aggressive VMware pricing risks longer-term customer losses to Nutanix and others.
Bottom line: Broadcom is a profitable, diversified infrastructure leader rather than a speculative bet. For a newer investor, the real questions are about price (paying ahead for growth), concentration (few customers), and temperament (it's volatile) — and whether it fits as one position within a diversified mix.
Glossary — terms used above
Market cap
The company's total stock-market value (share price × shares outstanding).
Hyperscaler
A giant cloud operator (Google, Amazon, Microsoft, Meta) running massive data centers.
Fabless
Designs chips but outsources manufacturing to a foundry such as TSMC.
ASIC / XPU
A custom chip purpose-built for one customer's specific workload.
Free cash flow
Cash generated after capital spending — what's available for dividends, buybacks, debt.
Payout ratio
The share of profit paid out as dividends; lower leaves more room to raise it.
Buyback
A company repurchasing its own shares, which can increase the value of remaining shares.
Beta
A volatility gauge; above 1.0 means it tends to move more than the overall market.
