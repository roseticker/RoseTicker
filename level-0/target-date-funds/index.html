"I'm in the 2055 fund." What is that?

If you peeked at your 401(k) or TSP and saw your money in something like a "2055 Fund" or "Lifecycle 2050," good news: that's a target-date fund, and it's one of the smartest defaults out there. It's a whole diversified portfolio in a single pick — designed so you can do nothing and still be invested sensibly.

The one-sentence version
A target-date fund is a ready-made, diversified portfolio in a single fund — you pick the one with the year closest to your retirement, and it automatically holds a sensible mix of stocks and bonds, getting gradually safer as that year approaches. For most beginners, it's the closest thing to a "just handle it for me" button.

02 · The big idea

A portfolio in a single fund

Normally, investing means choosing a mix of things — some stocks, some bonds, US and international — and adjusting it over the years. A target-date fund does all of that for you, inside one fund.

Imagine ordering a meal-kit box instead of shopping for every ingredient. The box already has the right proportions, and it even adjusts the recipe as your tastes change. That's a target-date fund: a complete, balanced mix in one tidy package.

When you buy a single "2055 Fund," under the hood you actually own a slice of thousands of companies plus some bonds — diversification that would take several funds to build yourself.

Why beginners love it
The two ways people hurt their returns are (1) not being diversified and (2) fiddling at the wrong moments. A target-date fund quietly solves both — it's diversified on day one and it adjusts on a schedule, not on your emotions.
What's inside one fund
A simplified peek under the hood of a "2055" fund
  • US stocks — the biggest slice when retirement is far off.
  • International stocks — companies outside the US, for broader reach.
  • Bonds — steadier, lower-growth ballast that grows as you near the date.
  • Auto-rebalancing — the fund keeps these in their target proportions for you.
03 · Decoding the year

What the "2055" actually means

The number is roughly the year you expect to retire — and you generally pick the fund closest to it. That's the entire selection process.

A simple way to find your year: take the age you'd like to retire (many people use ~65) and figure out which calendar year you'll hit it. Then pick the fund with the nearest year.

A quick example
If you're 32 in 2026 and imagine retiring around 65, that's roughly the year 2059 — so you'd choose the 2060 Fund (the closest available, since they usually come in 5-year steps). That's it. You've just built a complete, age-appropriate portfolio.

Prefer a more cautious or more aggressive ride? You can pick a fund with an earlier year (safer sooner) or a later year (more growth, more bumps) on purpose.

Find your fund in 10 seconds
  • Step 1: Pick a target retirement age (65 is a common anchor).
  • Step 2: Add the years between now and then to this year.
  • Step 3: Round to the nearest fund (they come in 5-year jumps: 2050, 2055, 2060…).
  • Step 4: That's your fund. Done.
04 · The clever part

The "glide path" — getting safer on autopilot

When retirement is decades away, the fund leans into growth (more stocks). As your date nears, it automatically shifts toward stability (more bonds). You never lift a finger.

How the mix shifts over a lifetime
Stocks (growth) Bonds (stability)
Illustrative shape only — every fund family designs its glide path a little differently. The point is the direction: aggressive when you're young and can ride out dips, gradually steadier as you approach the day you'll actually need the money. It does this on a schedule, immune to whatever the headlines are screaming.
05 · For federal & military readers

The TSP version: Lifecycle (L) funds

If you're in the Thrift Savings Plan, your target-date funds are called Lifecycle funds — "L 2055," "L 2050," and so on. Same idea, government edition.

A TSP L fund automatically blends the TSP's underlying funds — the stock funds (C, S, I) for growth and the steadier funds (G, F) for stability — and shifts that mix toward safety as your target year approaches. Exactly like a civilian target-date fund, with the TSP's famously low fees.

Good to know
Since 2015, new federal employees are automatically placed in an age-appropriate L fund (rather than the ultra-safe G Fund, which was the old default). So if you were hired more recently and never chose anything, there's a good chance you're already in a sensible L fund. Worth confirming — log in and check.
The TSP building blocks
What an L fund mixes together for you
  • G Fund — government securities. Very safe, very slow.
  • F Fund — bonds. Steadier ballast.
  • C Fund — large US companies (think S&P 500).
  • S Fund — smaller US companies.
  • I Fund — international companies.
  • L Funds — auto-blends all of the above by your target year.
06 · The honest fine print

Three things worth knowing

Target-date funds are excellent, but they're not magic. A few things are worth a glance so you're choosing on purpose, not by accident.

  • Check the fee. A target-date fund built from index funds is cheap (often around 0.1% or less). Some older or insurance-flavored versions cost far more — and high fees quietly eat returns over decades. If your plan offers a low-cost index target-date series, that's usually the one to favor.
  • It's meant to be your whole portfolio. The mix is already balanced, so you generally pick one and put everything there. Holding a target-date fund plus a pile of other funds can accidentally undo its careful balance.
  • The default year may not match your plan. Same year, different fund families can be more or less aggressive — and "to" versus "through" designs treat the years after retirement differently. You don't need to master this, just know you're allowed to pick an earlier (safer) or later (bolder) year if the default doesn't fit your comfort.
The bottom line
For a beginner who wants to be invested sensibly and then get on with life, a low-cost target-date fund is one of the best single decisions available. Pick your year, check the fee, make it your one holding, and let it do its job.
07 · Plain-English glossary

Jargon, translated

Target-date fund
An all-in-one fund built around a retirement year (like "2055"). Holds a full, diversified mix and adjusts it for you over time.
Glide path
The pre-set schedule by which the fund shifts from mostly stocks (growth) to more bonds (stability) as the target year nears.
Stocks
Slices of companies. Higher growth over time, but bumpier. The engine of a young person's portfolio.
Bonds
Loans to governments or companies that pay interest. Steadier and lower-growth — the ballast that smooths the ride.
Diversification
Not putting all your eggs in one basket — owning many investments so no single one can sink you.
Rebalancing
Periodically nudging your mix back to its targets as markets move. A target-date fund does this automatically.
Expense ratio
The fund's annual fee, as a percent. 0.10% means $1 a year per $1,000. Lower is better; it compounds.
Lifecycle (L) fund
The TSP's name for its target-date funds (L 2055, etc.). Same concept, with the TSP's very low fees.
"To" vs "through"
Whether a fund keeps shifting safer for years after the target date ("through") or stops at it ("to"). A design detail, not a beginner worry.
Asset allocation
The fancy term for your mix of stocks vs. bonds (and more). A target-date fund picks and manages it for you.
