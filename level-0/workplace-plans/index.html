401(k), 403(b), 457, TSP — what's the difference?

They sound like robot model numbers, and the alphabet soup is genuinely confusing. But they're all variations on one simple idea — a tax-friendly bucket for retirement savings. Here's what each one is, who gets which, and the handful of differences that actually matter.

The one-sentence version
A 401(k), 403(b), 457(b), and the federal TSP are the same basic thing — a workplace account that lets your retirement savings grow with a big tax break — just with different names depending on whether you work for a company, a nonprofit, a school, or the government. The differences are mostly in the fine print.
01 · If you only have a minute

The short version

✓

What's true of all of them

  • They're tax-advantaged buckets — the government rewards you for saving.
  • Money comes out of your paycheck automatically.
  • In 2026 you can contribute up to $24,500 (more if you're 50+).
  • Many come with free employer money (a match) on top.

Where they differ

  • Who gets which depends on your employer type.
  • 457(b) has a friendly quirk: no early-withdrawal penalty after you leave the job.
  • The TSP (federal) has famously low fees and simple fund choices.
  • Some let you stack two plans (e.g. a 403(b) and a 457).

What to focus on

  • Get your full employer match first.
  • Decide Roth vs traditional (tax now or tax later).
  • Remember the bucket is empty until you pick an investment.

02 · The shared idea

Why these exist at all

Strip away the numbers and letters, and every one of these is the government saying: "save for your own retirement, and we'll cut you a tax deal for it."

Left alone, most people under-save for retirement. So the tax code offers a bribe: put money in one of these accounts and you get a tax break — either now or later (more on that below).

In exchange, there's a catch: it's meant for retirement, so pulling it out early usually means taxes and a penalty. That "lock" is a feature — it keeps future-you funded.

The names are just job labels
401(k) = most private companies. 403(b) = schools, hospitals, nonprofits. 457(b) = state/local government and some nonprofits. TSP = federal employees and the military. Same engine, different badge.
The tax break, in one breath
  • Traditional: you skip taxes on the money now, and pay them when you withdraw in retirement.
  • Roth: you pay taxes now, and everything comes out tax-free in retirement — including decades of growth.
  • Either way, while the money sits invested, its growth isn't taxed year to year — which is a big deal over decades.
03 · Meet the family

The four, side by side

Same DNA, slightly different personalities. Find the one that matches your job.

Plan Who usually has it Worth knowing
401(k) Private-sector / company employees The most common one. Often comes with an employer match. Quality and fees vary a lot by employer.
403(b) Public schools, universities, hospitals, nonprofits Nearly identical to a 401(k). Watch for higher-fee insurance-style options in some plans — favor low-cost index funds.
457(b) State & local government, some nonprofits The friendly quirk: no 10% early-withdrawal penalty once you leave that employer. Can sometimes be stacked on top of a 403(b).
TSP Federal employees & military The government's own plan. Rock-bottom fees, a short menu of simple funds (G, F, C, S, I) plus all-in-one Lifecycle (L) funds. Usually paired with a FERS pension.
Public-sector readers, a note
If you work in government or public service, you may have a pension and one of these savings plans (classic example: federal FERS pension + TSP). They're separate things working together — the pension is a promised monthly check; the savings plan is your own invested pot. Understanding both is worth an afternoon.
04 · How much you can put in

2026 contribution limits

These are the most-you're-allowed numbers, set by the IRS and updated each year. You don't have to hit them — most people don't — but here's the ceiling.

$24,500
Your own contribution
The most you can personally put into a 401(k), 403(b), 457(b), or TSP in 2026.
+$8,000
Catch-up, age 50+
An extra allowance once you turn 50 — bringing the total to $32,500.
+$11,250
Super catch-up, ages 60–63
A larger catch-up in those four years (instead of the $8,000), under the SECURE 2.0 law.
Two more numbers, while we're here
A separate IRA (an account you open yourself, outside work) lets you add up to $7,500 in 2026, plus $1,100 more if you're 50+. And a heads-up for higher earners: starting in 2026, if your prior-year wages topped $150,000, any catch-up contributions must go in as Roth. Not something to worry about for most people just getting started.
05 · The one real decision

Roth or traditional?

This is the choice that actually matters, and it comes down to a single question: do you want your tax break now, or later?

Traditional
Tax break now
  • Your contribution is not taxed this year — it lowers your taxable income now.
  • You pay ordinary income tax when you withdraw in retirement.
  • Often favored if you expect to be in a lower tax bracket later.
Roth
Tax break later
  • You contribute after-tax money — no break this year.
  • Everything comes out completely tax-free in retirement, growth included.
  • Often favored when you're early-career and your tax rate is relatively low now.
If you can't decide
Many people early in their careers lean Roth, on the bet that paying tax at today's rate is a deal they'll be glad they took. But there's no universally "right" answer — it depends on your income now versus later. A fuller plain-English walkthrough is a good next read (coming in this series). You can also split contributions between both.
06 · Don't forget

The plan is still just the bucket

Picking the right account is step one. But an account on its own doesn't grow your money — what you put inside it does.

It's worth repeating, because it's the single most common gap: enrolling in a 401(k) or TSP sets up the container. Your money only grows once it's invested in something inside that container — usually a target-date fund or an index fund.

So once you've sorted out which plan you have and whether you're going Roth or traditional, the very next thing to check is what your money is actually invested in.

Your order of operations
  • 1. Contribute at least enough to get the full employer match.
  • 2. Choose Roth or traditional.
  • 3. Make sure the money is invested, not sitting in cash.
  • 4. Automate it, then leave it alone for years.
07 · Plain-English glossary

Jargon, translated

401(k)
A workplace retirement account for most private-company employees. Named after a section of the tax code (riveting, we know).
403(b)
The school / hospital / nonprofit version of a 401(k). Works almost identically.
457(b)
The state & local government version. Its perk: no early-withdrawal penalty once you leave that job.
TSP
Thrift Savings Plan — the federal government and military's retirement plan. Very low fees, very simple fund menu.
Tax-advantaged
A government-blessed account that gives you a tax break for saving — either now (traditional) or later (Roth).
Traditional
Skip the tax now, pay it when you withdraw in retirement.
Roth
Pay the tax now, withdraw everything tax-free later — growth included.
Contribution limit
The most you're allowed to put in per year, set by the IRS. $24,500 for these plans in 2026.
Catch-up contribution
Extra room to contribute once you're 50+ (and even more at ages 60–63), to help you make up ground.
IRA
Individual Retirement Account — one you open yourself, separate from work. Smaller limit ($7,500 in 2026), more fund choice.
FERS
Federal Employees Retirement System — the package most federal workers get: a pension, Social Security, and the TSP.
Pension
A promised monthly payment in retirement, funded by your employer. Common in public-sector jobs; separate from your savings plan.
