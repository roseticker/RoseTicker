Roth or traditional?

Your retirement plan asks you to pick "Roth" or "traditional," and it sounds like a trick question. It isn't. It's one simple trade — pay tax now, or pay it later — and once you see it that way, the choice gets a lot less scary.

The one-sentence version
Roth means you pay taxes on the money now and it comes out tax-free in retirement; traditional means you skip the tax now and pay it when you withdraw later. Early in your career, when your income (and tax rate) is relatively low, Roth is often the popular pick — but there's no wrong answer you can't adjust.

02 · The core idea

It's a bet about your tax rate

The government will tax this money at some point. The only question is whether you'd rather pay at today's rate or your future one.

Picture a farmer choosing when to pay tax: on the seed, or on the harvest.

Roth is paying tax on the seed — a small, known amount now — and then the entire harvest is yours, untaxed. Traditional is skipping the tax on the seed and paying it on the whole grown harvest later.

Why "early career" tilts Roth
If your income is modest now, your tax rate is probably lower than it'll be at your peak earning years. Paying tax now (Roth) locks in that low rate and shelters decades of growth from ever being taxed. That's the logic behind the common "go Roth while you're young" advice.
The growth is the prize
Why the Roth tax-free part can be a big deal

Say $6,000 of contributions grows into $40,000 over decades. With a Roth, that whole $40,000 is yours, tax-free. With traditional, you'll owe income tax on the full $40,000 when you take it out. You skipped tax on the small seed but pay it on the big harvest.

Neither is a scam — traditional's upfront break can be reinvested and is worth more if your future rate is genuinely lower. It's a real trade, not a trap.

03 · Side by side

The two, head to head

Same account, same investments inside it — the only difference is when the tax happens.

Roth
Tax the seed
  • This year: no tax break — you contribute after-tax dollars.
  • While invested: grows with no yearly taxes.
  • In retirement: withdrawals are 100% tax-free.
  • Best when: you think your tax rate is lower now than later.
  • Bonus: what you see is what you get — no future tax bill lurking.
Traditional
Tax the harvest
  • This year: lowers your taxable income — a break now.
  • While invested: grows with no yearly taxes.
  • In retirement: withdrawals taxed as ordinary income.
  • Best when: you think your tax rate will be lower in retirement.
  • Note: the upfront break is only an advantage if you actually invest it, not spend it.
04 · How to actually decide

A simple way to choose

You can't predict future tax law perfectly, and you don't have to. Here's a sane, beginner-friendly way to land on an answer.

  1. Get the match first — this comes before the Roth question. Whatever you pick, contribute enough to capture your full employer match. That free money dwarfs the Roth-vs-traditional decision.
  2. Early career or lower income right now? Lean Roth. You're likely paying a low tax rate today, so locking it in and getting decades of tax-free growth is a strong default.
  3. High income now and expecting less in retirement? Traditional has a real case. The upfront tax break is worth more when your current rate is high — just be sure you invest the savings, not spend them.
  4. Truly unsure? Split it. Many plans let you send part of each contribution to Roth and part to traditional. That hedges your bet and gives you both tax-free and taxable money in retirement — which is genuinely useful flexibility.
  5. Remember it's not permanent. You can change the Roth/traditional setting for future contributions whenever you like. This is a steering wheel, not a one-time door.
05 · A little fine print

Two things worth knowing

You don't need to master the tax code, but these two facts save people confusion later.

Workplace Roth vs. Roth IRA

"Roth" shows up in two places. The Roth option inside your 401(k), 403(b), or TSP has no income limit — anyone in the plan can use it. A Roth IRA (one you open yourself) is different: it has income limits, so high earners may be restricted or shut out. Same tax idea, different rulebooks.

Two facts about traditional money
  • RMDs: traditional accounts eventually force you to start withdrawing (and paying tax) in your 70s. Roth accounts are friendlier here.
  • 2026 high-earner rule: if you earned over $150,000 last year, your catch-up contributions (age 50+) must go in as Roth. Not a concern for most people starting out, but good to know.
The honest bottom line
For most people early on, "go Roth, capture the full match, and don't agonize" is a perfectly good answer. The difference between a reasonable choice and the theoretically optimal one is small compared to simply contributing and staying invested. Don't let this decision stall you.
06 · Plain-English glossary

Jargon, translated

Roth
Pay tax on the money now; withdraw it all tax-free in retirement, growth included.
Traditional (pre-tax)
Skip the tax now (lowering this year's bill); pay ordinary income tax when you withdraw later.
After-tax dollars
Money you've already paid income tax on. Roth contributions are made with these.
Tax bracket / rate
The percentage of income you pay in tax. The Roth-vs-traditional bet is really about your rate now vs. later.
Roth IRA
A Roth account you open yourself, outside work. Same tax perk, but with income limits.
RMD
Required Minimum Distribution — the age (currently 70s) at which traditional accounts force taxable withdrawals. Roth accounts are exempt for the original owner.
Tax-sheltered growth
Your money grows without being taxed year to year inside the account — true for both Roth and traditional.
Split contribution
Sending part of each paycheck's contribution to Roth and part to traditional — a way to hedge.
