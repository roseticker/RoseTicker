What's an index fund?

"Just buy an index fund" is the most repeated advice in investing — and almost no one explains what it actually means. Here it is in two minutes: instead of betting on which companies will win, you quietly buy a tiny slice of all of them.

The one-sentence version
An index fund is a single fund that automatically owns a whole list ("index") of companies — like the 500 biggest U.S. companies — so with one purchase you own a sliver of all of them, at very low cost, with no one trying (and usually failing) to pick winners for you.

The short version

Why people love them

  • Instant diversification — one fund, hundreds or thousands of companies.
  • Very low fees — no pricey manager to pay.
  • No stock-picking — you own the whole list, winners and all.
  • Beats most pros over the long run, simply by costing less.
≡

What "index" means

  • An index is just a published list of companies (e.g. the S&P 500).
  • An index fund simply owns everything on that list.
  • It rises and falls with the market, not a guru's hunches.
  • Bigger companies get a bigger slice — automatically.
!

Worth knowing

  • It won't beat the market — it is the market.
  • It still drops in downturns; diversification isn't immunity.
  • Check the fee — lower is better, and they vary.
  • Many target-date funds are built from index funds.
02 · The big idea

Buy the haystack, not the needle

Picking the one company that'll soar is hard — even experts mostly miss. An index fund skips the guessing entirely.

There's an old line: "Don't look for the needle in the haystack. Just buy the haystack."

Instead of trying to find the next Apple, an index fund buys a piece of every company on a list. Some will flop, some will soar — and the fund automatically holds more of the winners as they grow. You capture the success of the whole group without needing to predict which is which.

Index vs. "active" funds
An active fund pays a manager to hand-pick stocks and try to beat the market — and charges you more for it. An index fund just tracks the list, cheaply. Decades of data show that after fees, most active managers underperform the simple index they're trying to beat.
One fund, many companies
What buying a U.S. index fund gets you
  • A slice of Apple, Microsoft, your bank, a soda maker, a biotech you've never heard of — all at once.
  • No single company can sink you, because you own so many.
  • It rebalances itself as companies rise and fall.
  • You do nothing — that's the point.
03 · Why it quietly wins

The secret weapon is low fees

It sounds too boring to matter, but cost is the single biggest reason index funds beat most alternatives over time.

A typical active fund might charge around 0.85% a year. A broad index fund might charge 0.03%. That gap looks tiny — but it's skimmed off your entire balance, every year, for decades.

Over a long career, that difference can quietly cost tens of thousands of dollars. And here's the kicker: you're usually paying more for worse results, since most active funds don't beat the index anyway.

The three quiet advantages
  • Low cost — more of your money stays invested and compounding.
  • Diversification — owning everything smooths out single-company disasters.
  • No key-person risk — there's no star manager who can lose their touch or leave.
04 · Names you'll hear

Common index funds, briefly

You don't need to memorize these — just recognize them when they show up in your plan.

By what they track
  • S&P 500 index — the ~500 biggest U.S. companies. The classic "the market" benchmark.
  • Total U.S. market — basically every U.S. public company, large to small.
  • International — companies outside the U.S., for broader reach.
  • In the federal TSP, the C, S, and I funds are index funds (large U.S., smaller U.S., international).
"Index fund" vs. "ETF"?

You'll see both words. Most index funds come in two wrappers: a traditional mutual fund or an ETF (exchange-traded fund, which trades like a stock during the day). For a long-term beginner, the difference barely matters — both can track the same index at low cost. Don't let the label slow you down.

06 · Plain-English glossary

Jargon, translated

Index
A published list of companies that stands in for a market or slice of it — like the S&P 500.
Index fund
A fund that simply owns everything on an index, so you buy the whole list in one go.
Active fund
A fund where a manager hand-picks stocks to try to beat the market — usually at higher cost, often with worse results.
Expense ratio
The fund's annual fee, as a percent. 0.03% = $3/year per $10,000. Lower is better; it compounds against you.
Diversification
Spreading money across many investments so no single one can wreck you.
ETF
Exchange-traded fund — a fund that trades like a stock. Many index funds come in this form.
Mutual fund
The traditional fund wrapper, priced once a day. Index funds come in this form too.
The market
Shorthand for the overall stock market. An index fund aims to match it, not beat it.
