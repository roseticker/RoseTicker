The employer match: free money, really

If your job offers to "match" your retirement contributions, that's your employer handing you extra pay just for saving — the rare deal in finance that's genuinely too good to pass up. Here's how it works, and how to make sure you're getting all of it.

The one-sentence version
An employer match is extra money your employer adds to your retirement account when you contribute — often dollar-for-dollar up to a few percent of your pay. Contributing at least enough to get the full match is usually the single highest-value money move you can make, ahead of almost everything else.

02 · What a match actually is

Your employer chips in too

A match is a promise: "for every dollar you save (up to a limit), we'll add some of our own." It's compensation you only unlock by participating.

The most common formula sounds like: "We match 100% of the first 5% of your salary."

Translation: if you contribute 5% of your paycheck, your employer adds another 5%. You saved 5%, but 10% landed in your account. The other half came from them — free.

Common formulas you'll see
100% of the first 5% — they match every dollar up to 5% of pay. 50% of the first 6% — they add 50 cents per dollar up to 6% (so you contribute 6% to get a 3% match). Different shapes, same idea: contribute up to the threshold to collect it all.
Public-sector readers

Federal employees under FERS get a strong version: the government automatically adds 1% of your pay to your TSP even if you contribute nothing, then matches your contributions up to another 4%. Contribute 5% and the government adds a full 5%. Many state and local plans have their own match or pension-plus-savings setups — check yours, because the free portion is often generous.

04 · The cost of missing it

Why a few percent matters so much

Skipping the match isn't a small miss. It's free money you'll never get back — and it would have compounded for decades.

Say you leave $3,000 of match on the table every year for 10 years. That's $30,000 of free money missed — and because it never got invested, you also miss everything it would have grown into.

Invested over a long career, that missed match could have quietly become several times the original amount. No legitimate investment offers a guaranteed instant return like a match — which is exactly why it comes first.

The priority order
Where the match sits among money moves
  • 1. Contribute enough to get the full match. The guaranteed win.
  • 2. Pay down high-interest debt (like credit cards).
  • 3. Build a starter emergency fund.
  • 4. Then optimize — Roth vs traditional, extra contributions, an IRA.
  • The match is so good it usually jumps the line to the very top.
05 · The one catch

Vesting: when the match is truly yours

Your own contributions are always 100% yours. The employer's share sometimes comes with a waiting period before you fully own it.

Vesting is how long you must stay employed before the matched money is permanently yours to keep if you leave.

  • Immediate vesting: the match is yours right away. (The federal TSP match, for example, vests quickly.)
  • Cliff vesting: you own 0% until a date (say 3 years), then 100% all at once.
  • Graded vesting: you own a growing slice each year (e.g. 20% per year over 5 years).
Why it matters

Vesting only affects the employer's contributions, never your own. It's worth knowing your schedule before you change jobs — leaving a month before a vesting cliff could forfeit thousands in match. It's not a reason to avoid the match (you should still take it), just a date worth being aware of.

06 · Make sure you get all of it

Your 5-minute match checkup

A quick check that can be worth thousands of dollars a year.

  1. Find your match formula. Check your benefits portal, plan website, or ask HR: "What's our 401(k) match?" (or TSP/403(b)/457). Note the percentage and threshold.
  2. Check what you're contributing. Log into your plan and find your contribution rate. Compare it to the match threshold.
  3. Raise it to at least the full-match level if you can. Going from, say, 3% to 5% might cost you a little per paycheck but unlocks the rest of the free money.
  4. Watch the front-loading trap. If you max out early in the year, some plans stop matching once you hit the limit — unless they offer a "true-up." Spreading contributions across the whole year is the safe default.
  5. Note your vesting date so a job change doesn't accidentally cost you the employer's share.
07 · Plain-English glossary

Jargon, translated

Employer match
Money your employer adds to your retirement account when you contribute. Effectively free, extra pay.
Match formula
The rule for how much they add, like "100% of the first 5%" or "50% of the first 6%."
Match threshold
The contribution level you must reach to collect the full match. Contribute at least this much.
Vesting
How long you must stay employed before the employer's matched money is permanently yours.
Cliff vesting
You own 0% of the match until a set date, then 100% at once.
Graded vesting
You own a growing percentage of the match each year until fully vested.
True-up
An employer feature that pays any match you missed by hitting the contribution limit early. Not all plans have it.
FERS
Federal Employees Retirement System — includes an automatic 1% plus a TSP match up to 5% of pay.
