What you actually own

500 companies, one ticker

Buy one share of SPY and you own a slice of the 500 biggest companies in the U.S. — weighted by size, so the giants count more.

SPY follows the S&P 500, an index of about 500 of the largest American public companies. It's market-cap weighted: a $3-trillion company moves the fund far more than a $30-billion one. That's why the handful of mega-cap tech names — the "Magnificent Seven" — drive a big share of the returns.

The key point
SPY, VOO, and IVV all track this same index. On any given day they hold the same companies in the same proportions. You are not getting different stocks by choosing SPY — you're choosing a different wrapper around identical contents.
Roughly what's inside
Top concentrations, illustrative — S&P 500

  • Financials, health care, consumer — the next biggest slices
  • ~500 names total, but the top 10 carry outsized weight

The fee, and what it costs

0.0945% vs 0.03% — does it matter?

SPY costs about three times more than VOO or IVV. In percentage terms it's tiny. Over decades, it's real money — but smaller than people fear.

An expense ratio is the slice the fund company quietly takes each year. SPY keeps 0.0945% ($9.45 per $10,000); VOO and IVV keep 0.03% ($3 per $10,000). The difference is about $6.45 a year per $10,000 — which compounds because that money never gets to grow for you.

Over 30 years, on a one-time $10,000 investment growing ~7% a year, the fee gap costs SPY holders roughly $1,300 versus VOO/IVV. On $100,000, that's about $13,000. Real, but not life-changing — the bigger sin is paying it for no reason if you're just holding long-term.

Choose SPY if…

  • You trade actively — in and out over days or weeks, not years
  • You buy or sell options on the S&P 500 (SPY's options market is the deepest)
  • You move large amounts and need to trade instantly with tiny spreads
  • You want the most liquid index product on earth and don't mind the fee
●

Choose VOO if…

  • You're a long-term, buy-and-hold investor (most people)
  • You already invest at Vanguard (commission-free, default choice)
  • You're dollar-cost-averaging into retirement and want the lowest cost
  • It just crossed $1 trillion — the retail default for a reason
●

Choose IVV if…

  • You want VOO's low cost but invest at a non-Vanguard brokerage
  • You hold at Fidelity, Schwab, or similar where iShares is native
  • You like that it can reinvest dividends internally (like VOO)
  • Functionally a tie with VOO — pick by what's free at your broker
Bottom line
If you're holding for years, VOO or IVV — whichever is free at your brokerage. If you're trading or using options, SPY. The mistake is buy-and-hold investors paying SPY's premium out of habit.
SPY's real edge

Why traders love SPY

SPY's whole advantage comes down to one word: liquidity. Here's what that means, without the jargon.

Liquidity means how easily you can buy or sell something without moving its price. SPY trades tens of millions of shares a day, so there's always someone on the other side of your order — instantly, and at a fair price.

  • Tight bid-ask spread. The "spread" is the gap between the buy price and sell price — a hidden cost every time you trade. SPY's is razor-thin, so frequent trading costs you less.
  • The deepest options market. Options are contracts to buy or sell at a set price later — used to hedge or speculate. SPY has the most active options of any fund, so they're cheap to trade and easy to exit.
  • Always-on volume. Big institutions use SPY to move in and out of "the market" in seconds, which keeps it liquid for everyone.
Quick translations
The trader words, in plain English
  • Spread — the small gap between buy and sell prices. Narrower = cheaper to trade.
  • Volume — how many shares change hands. Higher = easier to trade big size.
  • Option — a contract betting on (or protecting against) a price move, without owning the shares.
  • Hedge — an offsetting trade that limits your losses if the market drops.
For most beginners
None of this matters if you're buying once a month and holding for years. That's the tell that you probably want VOO or IVV, not SPY.
The honest risk list

What can go wrong

SPY is a sensible, diversified fund — but "diversified" is not "safe." Here's the real risk picture.

It's still 100% stocks
Market risk
The S&P 500 has fallen 30–50% in past crashes (2008, 2020, 2022). SPY falls right along with it. Only invest money you can leave alone for 5+ years and watch drop hard without panic-selling.
Top-heavy concentration
Concentration
A handful of mega-cap tech names drive a large share of returns. When they stumble, the whole "diversified" fund feels it. You're more concentrated in big tech than the name "S&P 500" suggests.
The fee drag (vs alternatives)
Cost
Not dangerous, just unnecessary for long-term holders: you're handing over ~3× the fee of VOO/IVV for the identical index. Small each year, but it compounds.
U.S.-only
No global mix
SPY holds only large U.S. companies — no international stocks, no small caps. A globally diversified portfolio would add those separately.

Jargon, plain

The terms, decoded

ETF
Exchange-traded fund — a basket of many stocks you can buy and sell as a single share, all day, like a stock.
S&P 500
An index of ~500 of the largest U.S. public companies, weighted by size. The common shorthand for "the U.S. stock market."
Expense ratio
The yearly fee the fund keeps, as a percent of your money. 0.0945% = $9.45 per $10,000 per year.
Unit investment trust (UIT)
SPY's older legal structure from 1993. It can't instantly reinvest dividends, creating a tiny drag VOO and IVV avoid.
Liquidity
How easily you can trade without moving the price. SPY is the most liquid ETF in the world.
Bid-ask spread
The gap between the buy and sell price — a hidden trading cost. Narrower is cheaper; SPY's is very narrow.
Options
Contracts to buy or sell at a set price by a set date — used to hedge or speculate. SPY has the deepest options market.
AUM
Assets under management — the total money in the fund. SPY holds hundreds of billions; VOO just passed $1 trillion.
